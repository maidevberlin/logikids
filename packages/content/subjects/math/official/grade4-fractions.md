# Task Generation Guidelines for Fractions (Grade 4)

Generate tasks that help students understand fractions as parts of a whole, use fraction notation correctly, and compare simple fractions through visual and symbolic representations.

## Problem Structure Variations

Create diverse task types that test different aspects of fraction understanding:

1. **Part-Whole Identification**: Show a divided shape or set of objects where some parts are shaded or selected. Ask students to identify what fraction is represented, or conversely, show a fraction and ask them to identify which representation matches.

2. **Fraction Notation Practice**: Present situations (visual or contextual) and ask students to write the correct fraction using numerator and denominator. Or provide a fraction and ask students to identify which situation it describes.

3. **Fraction Comparison**: Give two or more fractions (with same denominators for {{difficulty}} easy, mixed denominators for {{difficulty}} medium) and ask which is larger, smaller, or if they're equal. Use both symbolic and visual representations.

4. **Equivalent Fraction Recognition**: Show different visual representations (circles divided into different numbers of parts, rectangles with different grids) and ask students to identify which fractions are equivalent. Focus on visual understanding rather than algorithmic conversion.

5. **Real-World Fraction Application**: Present everyday scenarios involving sharing, measuring, or dividing, and ask students to determine what fraction describes the situation. Examples include pizza slices, chocolate bars, groups of objects, time on a clock, or distances.

6. **Fraction Ordering**: Provide a set of fractions and ask students to arrange them from smallest to largest or vice versa. Use visual aids for younger ages, symbolic notation for older students.

7. **Missing Parts Problems**: Show a partially completed visual representation or word problem and ask students to determine what fraction is missing to make a whole, or what fraction has been used/removed.

8. **Fraction Creation**: Ask students to divide a shape or quantity into a specified number of equal parts and shade a given fraction. This reverses the typical identification task.

9. **Fraction Word Problems**: Present contextual problems requiring fraction understanding (e.g., "A cake is cut into 8 equal pieces. If someone eats 3 pieces, what fraction of the cake remains?").

10. **Unit Fraction Focus**: Emphasize understanding of unit fractions (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, etc.) as building blocks, asking students to combine unit fractions or compare their sizes.

## Age-Based Complexity Scaffolding

### Younger Students ({{age}} = 9)
- Focus heavily on visual representations (circles, rectangles, bars)
- Use common, familiar fractions (½, ¼, ⅓)
- Keep denominators small (up to 8)
- Use concrete, everyday contexts (pizza, chocolate, apples)
- Primarily comparison of fractions with same denominators
- Questions should be more qualitative ("Which is bigger?")
- Provide visual aids in task presentation

### Older Students ({{age}} = 10)
- Increase use of symbolic notation alongside visuals
- Introduce less common fractions (⅕, ⅙, ⅛, 1/10)
- Allow for larger denominators (up to 12)
- Use more varied contexts (measurement units, distances, groups of objects)
- Include comparison of fractions with different denominators (using visual support)
- Mix qualitative and quantitative questions
- Reduce reliance on visual aids, emphasize symbolic understanding

## Difficulty Scaling

### Easy
- Single-step fraction identification or comparison
- Common fractions only (½, ¼, ⅓)
- Visual representations always provided
- Same denominators for all comparison tasks
- Clear, unambiguous everyday contexts
- Direct questions with obvious answers

### Medium
- Two-step problems requiring reasoning
- Include fifths, sixths, eighths
- Mix of visual and symbolic representations
- Some comparison with different denominators (e.g., ½ vs. ⅓)
- More complex real-world scenarios requiring interpretation
- May require finding equivalent fractions using visuals

### Hard
- Multi-step problems combining concepts
- All common fractions including tenths and twelfths
- Primarily symbolic notation with minimal visual support
- Comparison requiring understanding of relative sizes
- Complex word problems with multiple fractions
- Problems requiring both equivalent fractions and ordering

## Numerical Value Variation Principles

**Denominators**: Vary systematically across common values - halves, thirds, fourths, fifths, sixths, eighths, tenths, twelfths. Avoid always using the same denominator patterns. Include both even and odd denominators.

**Numerators**: Use proper fractions (numerator less than denominator) as primary focus for grade 4. Vary the specific fraction values - don't repeatedly use 1/4, 3/4; also include 1/8, 5/8, 2/5, 4/5, etc.

**Quantities**: When using sets of objects or measurement contexts, vary the total quantities significantly - small groups (4-10 items), medium groups (12-24 items), and measurement units (centimeters, meters, hours, euros).

**Context Diversity**: Rotate through different real-world contexts - food items (not just pizza), groups of people or animals, time measurements, length measurements, collections of objects, parts of shapes. Avoid overusing any single context type.

## Visual Representation Guidelines

**Shape Variety**: Describe using circles, rectangles, bars, squares, triangles, and other polygons. Vary how shapes are divided - radially (like pie slices), in grids (like chocolate bars), in horizontal/vertical strips. Specify equal-sized parts clearly.

**Set Representations**: Describe showing groups of discrete objects (dots, stars, animals, fruits) where a subset is highlighted or colored differently to represent the fraction.

**Number Line Usage**: For older ages or higher difficulties, describe placing fractions on a number line between 0 and 1, helping students visualize fraction magnitude and ordering.

**Partitioning Descriptions**: Specify whether shapes should be divided equally, whether divisions should be visible, and which parts should be shaded or highlighted. Vary the orientation and arrangement of parts.

## Format and Presentation Guidance

**When to Use Visual Elements**:
- Always for {{age}} = 9 and {{difficulty}} = easy
- Frequently for {{age}} = 10 or {{difficulty}} = medium
- Optionally for {{difficulty}} = hard (to provide hints or verify answers)

**When to Use Tables**:
- When comparing multiple fractions systematically
- When organizing information about different scenarios
- When presenting fraction equivalencies

**LaTeX/Mathematical Notation**:
- Use inline notation for fractions in running text
- Use display notation for key fractions being compared or calculated
- Ensure fraction bars are clear and denominators/numerators are distinguishable

**Language Considerations**:
- Introduce and use correct terminology: numerator (Zähler), denominator (Nenner), fraction bar (Bruchstrich)
- Define "equal parts" clearly in all visual representations
- Use "parts of a whole" language consistently
- Avoid confusing terminology like "over" vs. proper fraction language

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**DO NOT**:
- Use improper fractions (numerator > denominator) - these are beyond grade 4 scope
- Include algorithmic fraction arithmetic (addition/subtraction of fractions) - grade 4 focuses on understanding, not computation
- Present fractions without clear visual or contextual support
- Use overly complex or abstract scenarios
- Assume students can convert between fractions algorithmically
- Mix decimals with fractions - keep concepts separate at this level
- Use non-unit fractions exclusively - ensure unit fractions (1/n) appear regularly

**Ensure Variety By**:
- Rotating through all problem structure types listed above
- Alternating between visual-primary and context-primary tasks
- Varying denominators and numerators systematically
- Using diverse real-world contexts and visual representations
- Balancing identification, comparison, and application tasks
- Including both recognition and production tasks
