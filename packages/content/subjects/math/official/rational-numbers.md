# Task Generation Guidelines for Rational Numbers (Grade 7, Hard Difficulty)

Generate tasks that help students develop deep understanding of the rational number system, computational fluency with mixed representations, and strategic competency in selecting appropriate methods for complex multi-step problems. Emphasis should be on connecting conceptual understanding to procedural fluency, reasoning about relationships between representations, and justifying mathematical thinking.

## Problem Structure Variations

Create diverse task types that systematically develop comprehensive rational number understanding:

1. **Rational Number Set and Ordering**: Present sets of rational numbers in mixed representations (fractions, decimals, percentages, ratios) and ask students to order them from least to greatest or to identify which is largest/smallest. Include both positive and negative rational numbers. Require explanation of reasoning strategy used (common denominator, decimal conversion, number line visualization, cross-multiplication). Include numbers where the ordering is not immediately obvious (e.g., -3/8, -0.3, -35%, -2/7 requiring careful comparison).

2. **Sign and Absolute Value Relationships**: Present rational numbers and ask about opposites, absolute values, and distance from zero on the number line. Include problems like "What is the opposite of -2.5?", "Which is farther from zero: -3/4 or 0.8?", "If a number has absolute value 2/5, what are the two possible numbers?". Require students to explain relationships using number line reasoning.

3. **Addition and Subtraction with Mixed Representations**: Present problems combining fractions, decimals, and whole numbers in single expressions (e.g., 3¾ + (-2.5) - 1⅜ = ?). Require students to choose whether to convert to common representation or work with mixed forms. Include both straightforward calculations and problems where strategic conversion choices significantly affect complexity. Include multi-step expressions requiring careful attention to order of operations and sign rules.

4. **Multiplication with Rational Numbers**: Create problems multiplying various combinations: fraction × fraction, fraction × decimal, decimal × decimal, including negative values. Include problems where strategic simplification before multiplication dramatically reduces computational difficulty. Include area model or area interpretations for some problems. Include problems that demonstrate counterintuitive results (multiplying by a proper fraction makes result smaller, multiplying by an improper fraction makes it larger, multiplying by negative numbers reverses sign).

5. **Division with Rational Numbers**: Present division problems with various combinations of representations and signs. Include "How many 3/4-liter servings in 2.4 liters?" and "Distribute -12.6 kg equally among 8 containers, what's the mass in each?" Include problems requiring multiplication by reciprocal understanding and sign rule application. Include interpretation of quotients in context.

6. **Multi-Step Operations with All Four Operations**: Create complex expressions combining addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division with rational numbers in mixed representations. Require careful order of operations application. Examples: (2/3 + 0.5) × (-1.2) - 3/4; 15.6 ÷ (-2.4) + 3/8 × 2. Include problems with parentheses requiring nested calculations.

7. **Conversion Between Representations**: Present rational numbers in one form and require conversion to other forms with explanation of process. Include terminating decimals converting to fractions, fractions converting to percentages with rounding, repeating decimals (0.3̄ = 1/3) requiring pattern recognition, percentages converting to fractions. Include identifying which representation is most useful for specific contexts.

8. **Percentage Problems in Context**: Present realistic scenarios involving percentage calculations: discounts and markups (item marked down from €50 by 15%), compound changes (first discount 20%, then additional 10% off), percentage of quantities (18% of 250 students), comparing percentages (which discount is better: 25% off or €15 off original €64.99?). Include problems where order matters (taking 20% off then adding 10% tax differs from adding 10% tax then taking 20% off).

9. **Rational Number Equations and Unknowns**: Present equations with missing rational numbers and ask students to solve. Include additive relationships (x + 3/8 = -1.5), multiplicative relationships (2/3 × x = -4.8), multi-step equations (3x - 1.5 = 7/2). Include verification that solutions are correct and discussion of whether solutions make sense in context.

10. **Coordinate System and Rational Numbers**: Present problems involving points with rational number coordinates, calculating distances between points on a coordinate plane, finding midpoints, and solving problems requiring movement with rational number vectors. Include both positive and negative coordinates, mixed representations, and real-world interpretations (temperature changes over time, elevation differences, profit/loss situations).

11. **Ratio and Proportion with Rational Numbers**: Present proportional reasoning problems using rational numbers (scaling recipes by fractional factors, converting between measurement systems using decimal conversion factors, comparing ratios expressed as fractions and decimals). Include direct proportion and inverse proportion problems. Include problems where setting up equations requires rational number operations.

12. **Absolute Value, Distance, and Inequalities**: Create problems involving absolute value expressions and their graphical representations, distance calculations between rational numbers on number lines, and solving/graphing simple inequalities with rational numbers. Include real-world contexts like tolerance ranges (measurement must be within ±0.3 cm) and temperature ranges.

13. **Number Property Applications**: Present problems requiring application of properties of rational number operations: demonstrating commutativity (a + b = b + a with rational numbers), associativity, distributivity (a(b + c) = ab + ac), properties of 0 and 1 (additive and multiplicative identities), and properties of opposites and reciprocals. Include explaining why a property holds using rational number examples.

14. **Complex Word Problems in Authentic Contexts**: Create multi-step problems set in realistic scenarios: financial (calculating net profit/loss involving both positive and negative values), scientific (temperature changes tracking both increases and decreases), geographic (elevation changes going up and down), measurement-based (fabric or material calculations with fractional and decimal quantities), and statistical (calculating averages and variations). Require identifying operations needed, working with mixed representations, and interpreting solutions in context.

15. **Estimation and Reasonableness**: Present calculations and ask students to estimate results before computing (e.g., "Estimate 9.8 × 3/4 before calculating exactly"). Include problems where estimation reveals calculation errors. Include deciding what level of precision is appropriate for different contexts (calculating ingredient quantities vs. scientific measurements).

## Age-Based Complexity Scaffolding

### Younger in Range ({{age}} = 12)
- Emphasize connections between representations and visual/concrete models
- Use smaller denominators and simpler decimal values initially
- Include step-by-step problem structure with explicit guidance on approaches
- Provide worked examples showing multiple solution strategies
- Use contexts students encounter frequently (money, cooking, sports statistics)
- Include visual representations (number lines, area models) for complex operations
- Focus on understanding why procedures work before rapid procedural fluency
- Keep most problems to 2-3 operations before combining with other concepts
- Explicitly teach and reinforce sign rules and absolute value concepts

### Older in Range ({{age}} = 13)
- Emphasize strategic choice of representations and solution methods
- Use broader range of denominators and decimal values
- Present less-structured multi-step problems requiring interpretation
- Minimize worked examples; require independent strategy development
- Use diverse contexts including more abstract mathematical scenarios
- Reduce reliance on visual representations; emphasize symbolic work
- Assume understanding of fundamental procedures; focus on application and justification
- Include complex expressions combining multiple operations
- Include discussion of multiple valid solution approaches and their relative efficiency

- Problems requiring justification of solution methods and explanation of mathematical thinking
- Emphasis on efficiency of approach and recognition of elegant solution strategies
- Inclusion of problems where the obvious approach is not the most efficient

## Numerical Value Variation Principles

**Fractions**: Vary systematically across proper fractions, improper fractions, and mixed numbers. Use denominators including: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12, and occasionally 7, 9, 11, 15 for challenging comparisons. Include both fractions where one denominator divides another and those requiring LCD calculation. Vary numerators to include unit fractions (1/n) and other fractions. Never repeat same fraction pairs in consecutive problems. Include fractions approaching zero (1/20) and fractions closer to 1 (9/10) to vary magnitude understanding.

**Decimals**: Use terminating decimals with varying decimal places (0.5, 0.75, 0.125, 0.625 for easier calculation; 0.3, 0.7, 0.37, 0.83 for harder). Include repeating decimals where pattern recognition is required (0.3̄ = 1/3, 0.6̄ = 2/3, 0.16̄ = 1/6). Vary decimal values across narrow ranges (between 0.1 and 0.9) and broader ranges. Use both very small decimals (0.01, 0.001) and larger ones (10.5, 100.25) to demonstrate place value understanding.

**Percentages**: Use percentages covering full range from less than 1% to greater than 100%. Include round numbers (25%, 50%, 75%) and more challenging values (33%, 37%, 87%). Include percentages describing decrease (using negative percentage change interpretation).

**Negative Values**: Systematically include negative fractions, negative decimals, and negative percentages. Create problems where sign operations are critical (adding negative + positive, multiplying negative × negative, etc.). Include problems contrasting positive and negative values to highlight number line relationships.

**Quantities in Context**: Vary significantly across problems - small values (±0.25, ±1/8), medium values (±5.75, ±3¾), large values (±125.5, ±42⅜). Avoid clustering around convenient values; include realistic messy quantities. Use both values that produce "clean" answers and those that require rounding or result in repeating decimals.

**Ratio and Proportion**: When presenting ratios, vary from simple ratios (1:2, 3:4) to more complex ratios (5:12, 7:9). Include both rates and ratios, and problems comparing multiple ratios requiring common denominators or decimal conversion.

**Decimal Places in Operations**: When performing operations on decimals, vary the precision: multiplying whole number by decimal with 2 places vs. 3 places, dividing to different decimal precisions. Include situations where rounding is necessary and where exact values are important.

## Format and Presentation Guidance

### When to Use Visual Elements

**For {{difficulty}} = hard:**
- Use visuals selectively for verification or when problem structure genuinely benefits from visualization
- Emphasize symbolic notation and abstract relationships
- Include number lines for comparing rational numbers and understanding opposites/absolute value
- Include area models for multiplication visualization when developing conceptual understanding
- Provide coordinate plane diagrams for position and distance problems
- Use diagrams primarily for complex real-world problems requiring interpretation

### When to Use Tables

- Organizing equivalent forms of rational numbers (fraction, decimal, percentage columns)
- Presenting step-by-step solution processes for multi-step problems
- Comparing multiple rational numbers with their different representations
- Recording intermediate calculations in complex multi-operation problems
- Displaying proportional relationships and scaling factors
- Showing currency conversions or unit conversions with multiple steps

### When to Use Number Lines

- Visualizing addition and subtraction as movements on the number line
- Showing rational number positions between -2 and 2 (or appropriate range)
- Comparing rational numbers visually by placement
- Identifying rational numbers between two given values
- Demonstrating absolute value as distance from zero
- Showing solution sets for inequalities with rational numbers

### When to Use Area Models

- Showing multiplication of rational numbers as overlapping regions
- Demonstrating why multiplying by proper fraction produces smaller result
- Visualizing fraction multiplication before computing
- Understanding reciprocal relationships visually

## Language Considerations

Use precise mathematical vocabulary consistently, including German terminology for bilingual understanding:

- **Rational number** (Rationale Zahl) - number expressible as ratio of integers p/q where q ≠ 0
- **Rational number system** (Zahlbereich der rationalen Zahlen, ℚ) - the complete set of all rational numbers
- **Equivalent rational numbers** (Äquivalente rationale Zahlen) - different representations of same quantity
- **Opposite/additive inverse** (Gegenzahl/additives Inverses) - number that sums with original to equal zero
- **Reciprocal/multiplicative inverse** (Kehrwert/multiplikatives Inverses) - number that multiplies with original to equal one
- **Absolute value** (Betrag) - distance from zero on number line
- **Terminating decimal** (Abbrechender Dezimalbruch) - decimal with finite decimal places
- **Repeating decimal** (Periodischer Dezimalbruch) - decimal with repeating pattern
- **Numerator and denominator** (Zähler und Nenner) - parts of fraction notation
- **Order of operations** (Rechenreihenfolge/Vorrangregeln) - PEMDAS/BODMAS
- **Sign rules** (Vorzeichenregeln) - rules for operations with positive and negative numbers
- **Conversion** (Umwandlung) - changing between representation forms
- **Simplest form** (Grundform/gekürzte Form) - fraction reduced to lowest terms

Define terms explicitly when introducing new vocabulary, and use them consistently. Emphasize the conceptual meaning alongside procedural application.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**DO NOT:**
- Present rational number operations without explaining when and why different representations are useful
- Use only convenient values that produce "clean" answers; include problems with messy results and rounding requirements
- Focus exclusively on procedural calculation without conceptual understanding of rational number relationships
- Mix representations (fractions, decimals, percentages) indiscriminately without explicit conversion problems
- Treat sign rules as arbitrary procedures; explain using number line and context reasoning
- Present word problems without realistic complexity; include multi-step scenarios requiring interpretation
- Assume students understand reciprocals and multiplicative inverses; explicitly teach these concepts
- Use only positive rational numbers; systematically include negative values in operations
- Avoid problems where procedure choice matters; include problems where different approaches have different computational efficiency
- Overlook the importance of reasonableness checking; include estimation and verification problems
- Include problems where multiple different answers could be correct due to ambiguous wording
- Focus only on traditional algorithms; include alternative strategies and justification of their validity
- Neglect discussion of precision and rounding appropriateness in different contexts

**Ensure Variety By:**
- Rotating through all problem structure types listed above systematically
- Alternating between symbolic-primary and context-primary tasks
- Varying representations systematically (fractions only, decimals only, mixed representations)
- Including positive, negative, and mixed-sign problems balanced throughout
- Using diverse real-world contexts from financial, scientific, geographic, culinary, and statistical domains
- Balancing procedural practice with conceptual understanding tasks
- Including both recognition (identify the operation/representation needed) and production (perform the operation) tasks
- Combining single operations with complex multi-step problems
- Mixing straightforward calculations with problems requiring strategic representation choices
- Including both forward problems (calculate result) and inverse problems (find missing value)
- Varying problem complexity from manageable multi-step to genuinely complex scenarios

## Connection to Curriculum Standards

This concept aligns with the KMK Bildungsstandards für Mathematik, Sekundarstufe I (Secondary Level I), within the content areas "Zahlbereiche und deren Eigenschaften" (Number systems and their properties) and "Rechenoperationen und ihre Eigenschaften" (Arithmetic operations and their properties).

Specifically, Grade 7 students develop:
- **Conceptual competency**: Understanding the rational number system as the complete set of fractions and decimals, including negative values, and understanding relationships between different representations
- **Procedural fluency**: Performing all four operations with rational numbers in various representations with accuracy and efficiency, applying sign rules consistently, and converting between representations flexibly
- **Strategic competency**: Selecting appropriate representations and solution methods for specific problems, recognizing when different approaches have different efficiency, and choosing strategies based on problem structure
- **Reasoning competency**: Justifying why procedures work using properties of rational numbers, explaining solution methods verbally and in writing, comparing alternative strategies, and applying rational number understanding to solve authentic problems
- **Communication competency**: Using precise mathematical terminology, explaining mathematical thinking clearly, and presenting solutions with appropriate justification

Grade 7 work with rational numbers extends elementary fraction and decimal understanding to include: the complete rational number system including negative values, operations with mixed representations, sophisticated problem-solving in authentic contexts, and the conceptual foundation for proportional reasoning, rates, and algebraic equations in subsequent grades.
