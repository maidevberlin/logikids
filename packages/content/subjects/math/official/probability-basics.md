# Task Generation Guidelines for Introduction to Probability (Grade 6)

Generate tasks that help students develop intuitive understanding of probability as a measure of likelihood, experience the relationship between theoretical and experimental probability, and apply probability concepts to realistic scenarios.

## Problem Structure Variations

Create diverse task types that test different aspects of probability understanding:

1. **Probability Scale Recognition**: Present events and ask students to place them on a probability scale (impossible → unlikely → equally likely → likely → certain) or estimate probability on a 0-1 scale. Include everyday events (raining tomorrow, rolling a 1 on a standard die, drawing a red card from a deck), impossible events (rolling a 7 on a standard die), and certain events (the sun rising tomorrow).

2. **Counting Favorable and Total Outcomes**: Present a simple scenario (drawing from a bag, rolling dice, spinning spinners, flipping coins) and ask students to count favorable outcomes and total possible outcomes, then calculate probability as a fraction. Include problems where outcomes are equally likely and where they are not.

3. **Theoretical Probability Calculation**: Given information about equally likely outcomes, ask students to calculate the probability of specific events. Include single events (probability of rolling a 3 on a die), compound events with replacement (probability of rolling two 3s), and events from different chance devices (probability of drawing a specific card or color from a spinner).

4. **Interpreting Experimental Results**: Present data from a probability experiment (e.g., "We flipped a coin 100 times and got 47 heads") and ask students to: identify the experimental probability, compare it to theoretical probability, and explain why they differ. Include various numbers of trials to show convergence with large samples.

5. **Designing Fair Games**: Given a game setup, ask students to determine if it's fair and why. Include games where outcomes are equally likely and others where they are not. Ask students to modify unfair games to make them fair, or to predict who has the advantage in a given game.

6. **Prediction and Forecasting**: Given information about probability, ask students to predict outcomes of future trials. Include problems like "If you spin this spinner 100 times, about how many times will it land on red?" and "After 10 trials, you got red 7 times. Do you think the spinner is fair?"

7. **Probability Language Usage**: Present scenarios and ask students to use precise probability language. Include interpreting statements like "impossible," "certain," "very likely," "equally likely" and applying them correctly to new situations.

8. **Comparing Probabilities**: Present two or more events and ask which is more likely, less likely, or equally likely. Include visual representations (spinners, number cubes, bags with different proportions of objects) and numerical information.

9. **Simple Probability Word Problems**: Embed probability concepts in realistic contexts. Include problems about games (board games, card games, dice games), daily life (what's the probability it rains given weather patterns?), drawing from collections (reaching into a bag without looking), and fair allocation (dividing items randomly).

10. **Experimental Probability Design**: Ask students to design an experiment to test a probability claim or explore the likelihood of an event. Include describing the experiment, predicting outcomes, conducting trials, and analyzing results.

## Age-Based Complexity Scaffolding

### Younger Students ({{age}} = 11)
- Use simple probability scenarios with obvious outcomes (dice with 6 faces, coins, simple spinners, bags with 2-3 colors)
- Provide visual representations for all probability situations (pictures of items, spinner diagrams, color swatches)
- Use smaller numbers of trials for experiments (20-50 rather than 100+)
- Focus on comparing probabilities qualitatively before calculating numerical values
- Use whole number counts rather than fractions initially, moving to simple fractions (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6)
- Provide explicit step-by-step structure for problem-solving
- Emphasize concrete, touchable contexts (physical manipulatives, real objects)
- Use simple probability language and define it explicitly
- Include direct verification (students can physically check if spinner is fair by examining proportions)

### Older Students ({{age}} = 12)
- Work with more complex probability setups (two dice, more colors/options, less uniform distributions)
- Reduce reliance on visual representations, using numerical descriptions increasingly
- Work with larger numbers of trials (100+ trials) to observe convergence
- Calculate probabilities using fractions and decimals
- Compare multiple events and order by probability
- Analyze why experimental and theoretical probability differ
- Work with more abstract scenarios (probabilities from descriptions without physical devices)
- Understand that more trials generally lead to results closer to theoretical probability
- Consider multiple factors affecting probability (size, number, proportion)

## Difficulty Scaling

### Easy
- Single, equally likely outcomes (standard die, fair coin, simple spinner)
- Small number of favorable outcomes (e.g., "What's the probability of rolling a 6?" not "What's the probability of rolling an even number?")
- Experiments with 20-40 trials
- Simple fractions (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6) for probability
- Comparing two probabilities visually or numerically
- Recognizing impossible (0) and certain (1) events
- Interpreting experimental results with direct visual comparison to theoretical
- Using probability language for simple, familiar scenarios

### Medium
- Multiple favorable outcomes (e.g., "What's the probability of rolling an even number?")
- Unequal outcomes in visual representations (spinners with unequal sections, bags with different proportions)
- Experiments with 50-80 trials and analysis of deviation from expected
- Fractions including fifths, eighths, tenths where they arise naturally
- Understanding that more trials generally give results closer to theoretical probability
- Simple two-outcome compound experiments (two coins, coin and die)
- Designing modifications to games to change fairness
- Predicting approximate outcomes given probability information

### Hard
- Complex compound events (rolling two dice, multiple draws without replacement)
- Complex visual representations requiring proportion interpretation
- Large samples (100+ trials) and statistical analysis of deviation
- Reducing fractions to understand equivalent probabilities
- Identifying when outcomes are not equally likely from given information
- Understanding conditional probability intuitively ("If you already drew one red ball, does that change the probability of the next draw?")
- Analyzing unfair games and calculating how unfair they are
- Complex word problems requiring multiple steps and probability reasoning

## Numerical Value Variation Principles

**Favorable and Total Outcomes**: Vary systematically - include 1 favorable outcome from 2-6 total outcomes, 2-3 favorable outcomes from larger pools, and situations where it's not immediately obvious how many favorable outcomes exist. Avoid clustering around simple fractions; include 2/5, 3/7, 3/8 alongside 1/2 and 1/4. Ensure students encounter both easy fractions to calculate and those requiring more thought.

**Probability Values**: Use a full range from 0 (impossible) through 0.5 (equally likely) to 1 (certain). Include very small probabilities (1/10, 1/12), moderate probabilities (1/3, 2/5, 3/8), and high probabilities (7/10, 5/6). Avoid only using "nice" probabilities like 1/2 and 1/4.

**Number of Trials**: Vary experiment sizes systematically - small (20 trials), medium (50 trials), large (100-200 trials) to show convergence. Vary results within expected ranges - sometimes closer to theoretical, sometimes with larger deviations, to keep students thinking about why.

**Experimental Results**: When presenting experimental results, sometimes they align closely with theory, sometimes they deviate significantly. Include both scenarios to build understanding that individual experiments vary but large numbers of trials converge. Include results that might prompt students to question fairness (e.g., 70 heads in 100 coin flips) and clearly fair results.

## Format and Presentation Guidance

### When to Use Visual Elements

**For {{age}} = 11 or {{difficulty}} = easy:**
- Include visual representations (spinner diagrams, bag illustrations, die faces, coin faces) for all probability scenarios
- Show colors, sections, and proportions explicitly
- Use pictures of objects being drawn from bags rather than symbolic notation
- Include visual representations of experimental data (picture graphs, tally marks)
- Show number lines marked 0, 1/2, 1 for probability scale

**For {{age}} = 12 and {{difficulty}} = medium:**
- Include visual representations for about 60% of tasks, especially for new concepts
- Use diagrams for compound events (two spinners side-by-side, two dice)
- Use tables and charts to record experimental results
- Allow increasingly symbolic representation (fractions without visual aid)
- Provide visual support for comparing probabilities

**For {{difficulty}} = hard:**
- Use visuals selectively - primarily for verification or when needed for clarity
- Emphasize numerical and symbolic reasoning
- Use tables for analyzing experimental data
- Include diagrams for complex compound events

### When to Use Tables

- Recording experimental results (Trial number, Outcome, Tally count)
- Organizing data from multiple experiments or conditions
- Comparing experimental results to theoretical predictions
- Displaying all possible outcomes for compound events (two dice outcomes in 6×6 table)
- Showing frequencies and relative frequencies for categorical data
- Tracking cumulative results over trials to show convergence

### When to Use Diagrams

- Spinner diagrams showing sections and their proportions
- Tree diagrams for compound events (two dice, two draws)
- Illustrations of bags/containers with colored objects
- Venn diagrams for "and/or" probability problems
- Pictographs or bar graphs for experimental results

### When to Use Number Lines

- Showing probability scale from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain)
- Placing specific probability values on a scale for comparison
- Illustrating intervals like "likely" (0.6-1) or "unlikely" (0-0.4)

## Language Considerations

Use precise probability language consistently:

- **Probability** (Wahrscheinlichkeit) - the measure of how likely an event is to occur
- **Event** (Ereignis) - a specific outcome or set of outcomes
- **Outcome** (Ergebnis) - a single possible result of a trial or experiment
- **Favorable outcome** (günstiges Ergebnis) - an outcome that represents the event of interest
- **Certain event** (sicheres Ereignis) - an event with probability 1 (will definitely happen)
- **Impossible event** (unmögliches Ereignis) - an event with probability 0 (cannot happen)
- **Equally likely** (gleich wahrscheinlich) - outcomes with the same probability of occurring
- **Theoretical probability** (theoretische Wahrscheinlichkeit) - calculated probability based on all possible outcomes
- **Experimental probability** (experimentelle Wahrscheinlichkeit) - observed frequency from trials
- **Fair** (gerecht/fair) - each outcome has equal probability
- **Unfair** (ungerecht) - outcomes do not have equal probability
- **Trial** (Versuch) - a single performance of an experiment
- **Experiment** (Experiment) - a process repeated multiple times to collect probability data

Define these terms explicitly and use them consistently throughout all tasks.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**DO NOT:**
- Present probability without connecting to real experiences and intuitions
- Use only scenarios where outcomes are equally likely; include varied distributions
- Skip the experimental component; students need hands-on experience
- Focus exclusively on calculation without understanding likelihood
- Present one-off results as proof of probability (e.g., "I flipped once and got heads, so probability is 100%")
- Assume students understand fractions are needed to express probability
- Use overly abstract or unfamiliar scenarios (stick to contexts students encounter)
- Include problems with ambiguous outcomes or misidentified favorable outcomes
- Skip comparing theoretical and experimental results; this is central to understanding
- Use only symmetric probability distributions; include varied proportions
- Avoid discussing when experimental results differ from theoretical predictions

**Ensure Variety By:**
- Rotating through all problem structure types listed above
- Alternating between visual-primary and numerical-primary tasks
- Varying probability values systematically across the full range 0-1
- Using diverse real-world contexts from games, weather, sports, fair allocation, and daily life
- Balancing single-outcome events with compound events
- Including both prediction tasks and analysis of results
- Mixing visual representations with increasingly symbolic notation
- Including both fair and unfair scenarios
- Varying experiment sizes to show convergence effects
- Including scenarios where intuition might mislead students
