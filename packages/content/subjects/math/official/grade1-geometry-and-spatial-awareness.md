# Geometry and Spatial Awareness

## Task Generation Guidelines

Generate tasks that help first-grade students (ages {{age}}) explore geometric shapes and spatial relationships through concrete, hands-on scenarios. Tasks should be playful and visual, connecting abstract geometric concepts to familiar objects and situations from children's everyday experiences.

### Problem Type Variations

Create diverse task structures that engage different aspects of geometric thinking:

1. **Shape Recognition**: Identify named shapes among a collection of objects or images
2. **Shape Sorting**: Group objects by shared geometric properties (number of corners, curved vs straight edges)
3. **Spatial Position Description**: Determine or describe the location of objects using directional vocabulary
4. **Shape Construction**: Count components needed to build or draw a specific shape
5. **Symmetry Detection**: Identify whether patterns, objects, or images have mirror symmetry
6. **Environmental Shape Hunt**: Find examples of geometric shapes in real-world contexts (playground, kitchen, nature)
7. **Shape Comparison**: Compare two or more shapes based on specific attributes (corners, edges, size)
8. **Position Following**: Interpret or give instructions using spatial vocabulary to locate objects
9. **Pattern Completion**: Extend or complete geometric patterns based on shape and position
10. **Shape Property Description**: Match shapes to descriptions of their characteristics

### Age-Appropriate Complexity

**For younger students ({{age}} = 6):**
- Focus on recognition and naming of basic shapes (circle, square, triangle)
- Use simple spatial terms (above, below, next to)
- Present clear, unambiguous visual examples
- Use familiar objects and contexts (toys, food, classroom items)
- Keep questions direct with minimal multi-step reasoning

**For older students ({{age}} = 7):**
- Introduce more complex shapes (rectangle, pentagon, cone, pyramid)
- Use combined spatial relationships (above and to the left of)
- Include slight variations in shape orientation or size
- Present more abstract scenarios requiring visualization
- Allow for simple two-step reasoning (find the shape, then count its corners)

### Difficulty Scaling

**Easy ({{difficulty}} = easy):**
- Single shape identification from clear images
- Basic spatial terms with obvious positions
- Symmetry in simple, familiar objects (butterfly, face)
- One attribute comparison at a time
- Common everyday objects as examples

**Medium ({{difficulty}} = medium):**
- Multiple shapes to identify or compare
- Combined spatial relationships (between, corner)
- Symmetry in patterns or arrangements
- Two attributes to consider simultaneously
- Less obvious real-world shape examples

**Hard ({{difficulty}} = hard):**
- Shapes in complex arrangements or unusual orientations
- Precise spatial descriptions requiring multiple terms
- Incomplete or partially hidden symmetry patterns
- Three or more attributes to analyze
- Abstract or stylized representations of shapes

### Visual Representation Guidelines

Tasks should include visual elements appropriate for first-grade learners:

- **For shape recognition tasks**: Show clear, colorful images of shapes in various orientations
- **For spatial relations tasks**: Present scenes with objects in different positions, using simple backgrounds to avoid distraction
- **For symmetry tasks**: Display objects or patterns with visible symmetry lines or folding axes
- **For environmental tasks**: Use photographs or illustrations of familiar settings (classroom, home, playground, nature)

Describe visual elements conceptually rather than providing specific drawing code. For example: "show a collection of five objects where three are spherical and two are cubic" or "display a simple house drawing with rectangular windows and a triangular roof."

### Contextual Variety

Draw from diverse, age-appropriate scenarios that resonate with first-graders:

- **Play contexts**: Toys, building blocks, balls, playground equipment
- **Home environments**: Furniture, dishes, food items, household objects
- **Nature**: Leaves, flowers, fruits, stones, animals
- **School settings**: Books, pencils, erasers, classroom materials
- **Food**: Fruits, vegetables, snacks with recognizable shapes
- **Transportation**: Wheels, windows, signs, vehicles
- **Art and crafts**: Drawings, paper shapes, patterns, decorations

### Parameter Variation Principles

**For object quantities**: Vary from single objects to small collections (typically 3-8 items for first grade)

**For shape complexity**: Progress from simple common shapes (circle, square) to more complex ones (pentagon, hexagon, cylinder, cone) based on age and difficulty

**For spatial scenarios**: Range from simple two-object arrangements to more complex scenes with multiple objects in various positions

**For symmetry examples**: Start with bilateral symmetry in single objects, advance to symmetry in patterns or arrangements

### Language and Vocabulary

Use clear, concrete language appropriate for first-graders:

- **Shape names**: circle, triangle, square, rectangle, cube, sphere, cylinder, cone, pyramid
- **Spatial terms**: above, below, left, right, next to, between, in front of, behind, inside, outside, on top of, under
- **Shape properties**: corner, edge, side, curved, straight, round, flat
- **Symmetry terms**: mirror, same on both sides, fold line, match

Avoid technical geometric terms like "vertices," "faces," or "congruent" - use child-friendly vocabulary.

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

- Using abstract or technical terminology beyond first-grade comprehension
- Presenting shapes in only one orientation (children should recognize rotated shapes)
- Assuming left-right orientation is automatic (this develops throughout early elementary years)
- Including too many objects or details in visual tasks (causes cognitive overload)
- Making symmetry tasks too complex (partial symmetry or multiple axes)
- Using unfamiliar objects or contexts that require additional explanation

### Special Considerations

**For visual tasks**: Ensure sufficient contrast and clarity for young learners; avoid cluttered backgrounds

**For spatial reasoning**: Remember that young children are still developing left-right discrimination; provide clear reference points

**For symmetry**: Start with vertical symmetry (easier to perceive) before horizontal or diagonal

**For shape properties**: Connect to tactile experiences (corners you can feel, edges you can trace)

**Cultural sensitivity**: Choose objects and contexts familiar across different cultural backgrounds when possible
