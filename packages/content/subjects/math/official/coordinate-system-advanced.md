# Task Generation Guidelines for Coordinate System and Linear Functions (Grade 7)

Generate tasks that develop students' ability to work fluently with coordinate systems, understand and calculate slope, interpret linear equations, and apply linear relationships to solve real-world problems.

## Problem Structure Variations

Create diverse task types that test different aspects of coordinate system and linear function understanding:

1. **Plotting Points in All Quadrants**: Give students ordered pairs and ask them to plot points on a coordinate grid. Include points in all four quadrants, on the axes, and at the origin. Vary the scale of axes (by 1s, 2s, 5s, 10s) to require careful attention to labeling and counting.

2. **Identifying Points on a Graph**: Show a graph with plotted points and ask students to identify the coordinates of specific points. Include points with negative coordinates, fractional coordinates, and points in each quadrant.

3. **Understanding and Calculating Slope**: Present two points and ask students to calculate slope using rise/run. Include problems where students identify slope from graphs (counting rise and run visually), from tables (comparing change in y to change in x), and from equations (recognizing m in y = mx + b).

4. **Graphing Linear Equations**: Give linear equations in various forms (slope-intercept y = mx + b, standard Ax + By = C, or point-slope form) and ask students to graph the line. Include equations with positive slope, negative slope, zero slope, and undefined slope.

5. **Finding Equations from Graphs**: Show a graph of a line and ask students to determine its equation. Include finding slope from the graph and y-intercept, then writing the equation in slope-intercept form.

6. **Finding Equations from Points**: Give two points on a line (or one point and the slope) and ask students to find the equation of the line. Require students to calculate slope from two points, then use point-slope form or substitute into y = mx + b.

7. **Interpreting Slope and Y-Intercept in Context**: Present a real-world situation (distance-time, cost function, temperature over time, height of a plant) with a linear equation or graph. Ask students to identify and interpret the slope (rate of change) and y-intercept (starting value) in the context of the problem.

8. **Creating Equations from Real-World Scenarios**: Describe a situation with a linear relationship (e.g., "A taxi charges $3 to start plus $2 per mile") and ask students to write an equation, identify slope and y-intercept, and make predictions.

9. **Distance Between Points**: Give two points and ask students to find the distance between them using the distance formula or by analyzing a right triangle formed by the points. Include both cases where the segment is aligned with axes and where it's diagonal.

10. **Analyzing and Comparing Linear Relationships**: Present multiple equations or graphs and ask students to compare their slopes, y-intercepts, and behaviors. Ask questions like "Which line is steeper?" "Which starts higher on the y-axis?" "Which will eventually be greater if we extend the lines?"

11. **Solving Problems Involving Intersecting Lines**: Present two lines (as equations or graphs) and ask students to find their intersection point. Interpret this point in context (e.g., when two cost functions are equal, when two objects meet).

12. **Predicting and Extending Patterns**: Given a table of values showing a linear relationship, ask students to find the pattern (slope), extend the table, write an equation, and make predictions beyond the given data.

## Age-Based Complexity Scaffolding

### Younger Students ({{age}} = 12)
- Use smaller coordinate grids with grid lines and labeled axes
- Limit slopes to simple values initially (m = 1, 2, -1, -2, 1/2, -1/2)
- Provide gridded backgrounds for all graphing tasks
- Focus on slope-intercept form (y = mx + b) initially, introduce other forms later
- Use whole number coordinates more frequently than fractions
- Provide step-by-step structure for finding equations from points
- Use concrete, familiar real-world contexts (video game characters moving, simple costs)
- Emphasize visual verification (counting rise and run on grids) before symbolic calculation
- Include units and scales clearly in all graphs
- Use smaller coordinate values (usually -10 to 10 range)

### Older Students ({{age}} = 13)
- Work with larger coordinate ranges and variable grid intervals
- Calculate slope from any two points without grid support
- Work fluently with fractional and negative slopes
- Convert between different equation forms (slope-intercept, standard, point-slope)
- Work with applications involving larger numbers and real-world context
- Analyze systems of equations and intersection points algebraically
- Work with less familiar or more complex scenarios
- Understand slope in abstract contexts beyond physical movement
- Use standard form and point-slope form with confidence
- Apply distance formula to non-axis-aligned segments

## Difficulty Scaling

### Easy
- Plotting and identifying points with positive integer coordinates only or on grids with clear marking
- Points primarily in Quadrant I initially, then introducing other quadrants one at a time
- Simple equations with slope m = 1, 2, or 1/2 and integer y-intercepts
- Graphs with clear grid lines and reasonable scale
- Slope calculated from obvious rise and run (e.g., up 2, right 1)
- Y-intercept directly visible on graphs
- Real-world contexts with straightforward relationships (constant speed, consistent hourly wage)
- Distance calculations between axis-aligned points or simple diagonal segments
- Equations in slope-intercept form primarily
- Small coordinate values (-10 to 10)

### Medium
- Plotting points with negative coordinates and fractional coordinates
- Points distributed across all four quadrants
- Equations with various slopes including negative slopes and non-unit fractions (m = 3/2, -2/3, -5/4)
- Lines that don't pass through obvious grid points
- Slope calculation from two arbitrary points
- Y-intercept that may be a fraction or negative
- Real-world contexts requiring setup of equations (distance-time problems, cost functions with fixed costs)
- Distance calculations using the distance formula or Pythagorean theorem
- Converting between slope-intercept and standard forms
- Coordinate ranges from -20 to 20
- Graphs without grid lines requiring axis labeling and scaling
- Interpreting slope and intercept in realistic contexts

### Hard
- Points with fractional and irrational coordinates
- Steeper slopes and negative slopes requiring careful scaling
- Multiple representations of the same line (different equation forms)
- Systems of two linear equations requiring intersection analysis
- Slope calculated from abstract contexts or tables without visual support
- Real-world scenarios with multiple constraints or unusual relationships
- Complex applications requiring model creation from descriptions
- Distance calculations with irrational distances (e.g., √13)
- Analysis of parallel lines (same slope) and perpendicular lines (negative reciprocal slopes)
- Larger coordinate ranges and variable scales
- Creating and interpreting equations from data tables with noise or gaps
- Using linear functions to make predictions and analyze the validity of models

## Numerical Value Variation Principles

**Slope Values**: Vary systematically across positive, negative, zero, and undefined slopes. Include slopes of 1, 2, 3, 1/2, 1/3, 2/3, 3/4, -1, -2, -1/2, -3/4, and slopes where rise and run have no common factors. Ensure students encounter both steep slopes (m = 3 or m = -3) and gentle slopes (m = 1/4 or m = -1/4). Include zero slope (horizontal lines) and undefined slope (vertical lines).

**Y-Intercept Values**: Use positive, negative, zero, and fractional y-intercepts. Include intercepts that are integers (-5, -2, 1, 3, 5) and non-integers (b = 1/2, -3/2, 2.5). Vary the position to avoid clustering at small positive integers.

**Coordinates**: When plotting points, use coordinates that include positive and negative values, integers and fractions, small values (-10 to 10) and larger values (up to 50 for real-world contexts). Include points at (-3, 4), (0, -2), (5, -7), (-1/2, 3), etc.

**Distances**: Include both axis-aligned distances (where only one coordinate differs) and diagonal distances. For diagonal distances, include those resulting in whole number distances (3-4-5 triangles, 5-12-13 triangles) and irrational distances (√5, √13, √37).

**Real-World Values**: Use realistic numbers for applications—taxi fares around $2-5 per mile with $2-5 starting fees, temperatures from -20°C to 40°C, distances from 0 to 500 miles, time intervals from 0 to 8 hours, heights from 0 to 200 cm. Ensure calculations remain reasonable and interpretable.

## Format and Presentation Guidance

### When to Use Coordinate Grids

**For {{age}} = 12 or {{difficulty}} = easy:**
- Provide printed coordinate grids with grid lines visible
- Label axes clearly with appropriate scale markings (1, 2, 5, or 10 unit intervals)
- Include grid size recommendation (e.g., "Use a grid where each square represents 1 unit")
- Show axis labels (x and y) and the origin marked
- Use clear, large graph paper style for ease of plotting
- Indicate scale on axes explicitly

**For {{age}} = 13 and {{difficulty}} = medium:**
- Provide coordinate axes with grid lines for some problems, blank axes for others
- Include axis labels and scale indicators
- Require students to determine appropriate scale for given points or ranges
- Mix gridded and non-gridded presentations
- Use standard Cartesian plane format

**For {{difficulty}} = hard:**
- Provide blank coordinate axes requiring students to label and scale appropriately
- Include problems requiring students to choose optimal scales
- Use varying scales on different axes when appropriate to contexts
- Require interpretation of non-standard graph presentations

### When to Use Tables

- Recording ordered pairs and their locations in specific quadrants
- Showing input-output (x-y) relationships from which patterns emerge
- Displaying multiple points used to determine a linear equation
- Comparing values from different linear equations at same x-values
- Recording calculations of slope (change in y, change in x)
- Organizing real-world data before graphing

### When to Use Diagrams

- Right triangles formed by horizontal and vertical distances (for visualizing rise and run)
- Multiple lines on one coordinate grid for comparing slopes and intercepts
- Distance diagrams showing how two points relate geometrically
- Labeled arrows showing rise and run on a graphed line segment
- Axes with quadrant labels (I, II, III, IV)

### When to Use Lists or Text

- Equations in various forms (y = mx + b, Ax + By = C, y - y₁ = m(x - x₁))
- Step-by-step solutions showing the process of finding equations
- Interpretation of slope and y-intercept in real-world context
- Comparison statements ("Line A is steeper than Line B because...")

## Language Considerations

Use precise coordinate system and linear function terminology consistently:

- **Coordinate system** (Koordinatensystem) - a system for locating points in a plane using ordered pairs
- **Ordered pair** (geordnetes Paar) - a pair of numbers (x, y) representing a point's location
- **x-coordinate** (x-Koordinate) - the first number in an ordered pair, indicating horizontal position
- **y-coordinate** (y-Koordinate) - the second number in an ordered pair, indicating vertical position
- **Quadrant** (Quadrant) - one of the four regions created by the intersection of the x and y axes
- **Origin** (Ursprung) - the point (0, 0) where the axes intersect
- **Axis / Axes** (Achse / Achsen) - the horizontal (x-axis) and vertical (y-axis) reference lines
- **Linear equation** (lineare Gleichung) - an equation whose graph is a straight line
- **Slope** (Steigung/Neigung) - the measure of a line's steepness, calculated as rise over run or (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁)
- **Rise** (Anstieg) - the vertical change between two points
- **Run** (Verlauf) - the horizontal change between two points
- **Positive slope** (positive Steigung) - a line that increases from left to right
- **Negative slope** (negative Steigung) - a line that decreases from left to right
- **Zero slope** (Steigung = 0) - a horizontal line with no vertical change
- **Undefined slope** (undefinierte Steigung) - a vertical line with no horizontal change
- **Y-intercept** (y-Achsenabschnitt) - the y-coordinate where a line crosses the y-axis (where x = 0)
- **X-intercept** (x-Achsenabschnitt) - the x-coordinate where a line crosses the x-axis (where y = 0)
- **Slope-intercept form** (Normalform/Steigungsform) - y = mx + b, where m is slope and b is y-intercept
- **Standard form** (Standardform) - Ax + By = C for linear equations
- **Point-slope form** (Punkt-Steigungs-Form) - y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)
- **Distance formula** (Abstandsformel) - d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]
- **Parallel lines** (parallele Linien) - lines with the same slope that never intersect
- **Perpendicular lines** (rechtwinklige Linien) - lines that intersect at a 90-degree angle
- **Intersection point** (Schnittpunkt) - the point where two lines cross
- **Graph** (Graph/Grafik) - a visual representation of a mathematical relationship
- **Plot** (darstellen/eintragen) - to mark points on a coordinate plane

Define these terms explicitly when first introduced and use them consistently throughout all tasks.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**DO NOT:**
- Mix up x and y coordinates (remembering x comes first: horizontal first, then vertical)
- Assume students automatically understand quadrant numbering; explicitly teach Roman numerals I-IV and their positions
- Use only positive coordinates; students need practice with negative coordinates in all quadrants
- Present slope only as a formula without visual understanding (rise over run on a graph is essential first)
- Use only "nice" slopes (m = 1, 2); include slopes like 2/3, 3/4, -5/4 that require careful counting
- Forget to discuss the meaning of slope in real-world contexts (what does the number actually represent?)
- Skip the y-intercept interpretation; students need to understand it as "the starting value" or "the amount when none of the independent variable is used"
- Assume students can convert between equation forms; scaffold this process
- Use scales that make it difficult to read coordinates accurately (e.g., having 1 unit = 3 mm on paper)
- Present only equations given in slope-intercept form; require finding equations from graphs and two points
- Use only axis-aligned distance problems; include diagonal distances
- Forget to discuss units in real-world problems (distance in miles, cost in dollars, time in hours)
- Create problems where the line doesn't pass through grid intersection points without acknowledging the challenge
- Skip checking if solutions make sense in context ("Does this answer answer the original question?")

**Ensure Variety By:**
- Rotating through all problem structure types listed above
- Alternating between graphing tasks (visual) and algebraic tasks (symbolic)
- Varying slopes systematically (positive, negative, zero, undefined, steep, gentle)
- Using diverse real-world contexts (motion, economics, natural phenomena, engineering, sports)
- Mixing points in different quadrants and on different axes
- Varying equation forms and requiring conversions
- Including both "find the equation" and "find the graph" tasks
- Alternating between gridded support and requiring student-provided axes
- Mixing calculation-focused and interpretation-focused problems
- Including problems where students create models and problems where they interpret given models
- Varying coordinate scales and ranges to develop flexible thinking

- Understanding and working with ordered pairs and coordinate systems in all four quadrants

Grade 7 understanding of coordinate systems and linear functions forms the foundation for more advanced algebra (systems of equations, quadratic functions), geometry (transformations, slopes of perpendicular lines), and applied mathematics in science, economics, and engineering. The ability to move fluidly between visual (graphical), symbolic (algebraic), numerical (tabular), and contextual (real-world) representations of linear relationships is essential for mathematical literacy and problem-solving in higher mathematics and STEM fields.
