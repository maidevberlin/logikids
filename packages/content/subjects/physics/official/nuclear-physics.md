# Nuclear Physics Tasks

Create physics problems that explore nuclear structure, nuclear reactions, and the enormous energies involved in nuclear processes. Problems should help students understand mass-energy equivalence, calculate binding energies and mass defects, compare fission and fusion, and evaluate nuclear technology critically.

**Vary the problem structure:**
- **Mass-energy calculations**: Apply Einstein's equation $E = mc^2$ where $c = 3 \times 10^8$ m/s. Calculate energy released when mass is converted to energy (1 u = 931.5 MeV/c²)
- **Mass defect and binding energy**: Calculate mass defect $\Delta m = Z \cdot m_p + N \cdot m_n - m_{\text{nucleus}}$ where proton mass $m_p = 1.00728$ u, neutron mass $m_n = 1.00867$ u. Convert to binding energy using $E_B = \Delta m \cdot c^2$
- **Binding energy per nucleon**: Calculate $\frac{E_B}{A}$ and explain nuclear stability - most stable nuclei (Fe-56) have highest binding energy per nucleon (~8.8 MeV)
- **Nuclear fission**: Analyze reactions like $^{235}_{92}U + ^1_0n \rightarrow ^{141}_{56}Ba + ^{92}_{36}Kr + 3^1_0n + \text{energy}$. Calculate energy release from mass difference
- **Chain reactions**: Explain how neutrons from fission trigger more fissions, concept of critical mass, role of moderators and control rods in reactors
- **Nuclear fusion**: Analyze reactions like $^2_1H + ^3_1H \rightarrow ^4_2He + ^1_0n + 17.6$ MeV. Explain why fusion requires extreme temperatures (overcoming Coulomb repulsion)
- **Energy comparisons**: Compare energy density - nuclear vs chemical reactions. Fission of 1 kg U-235 releases ~8×10¹³ J, while burning 1 kg coal releases ~3×10⁷ J (million times difference)
- **Quark structure**: Describe protons (uud) and neutrons (udd) as composed of up and down quarks held by strong nuclear force carried by gluons
- **Stellar processes**: Explain hydrogen fusion in Sun ($4^1_1H \rightarrow ^4_2He + 2e^+ + 2\nu_e + \gamma$), how stars produce elements up to iron

**Vary the content/context:**
- **Nuclear fission**: U-235, Pu-239, criticality, chain reactions, Manhattan Project, nuclear reactors (PWR, BWR), nuclear waste, accidents (Chernobyl, Fukushima)
- **Nuclear fusion**: Deuterium-tritium fusion, fusion in stars, Sun's energy source, tokamak reactors (ITER), inertial confinement, fusion as future energy
- **Nuclear weapons**: Fission bombs (atomic bombs), fusion bombs (hydrogen bombs), destructive power measured in kilotons/megatons TNT equivalent
- **Stellar nucleosynthesis**: Hydrogen burning (p-p chain), helium burning, carbon/oxygen fusion, supernova nucleosynthesis producing heavy elements
- **Particle physics**: Quarks (up, down, strange, charm, top, bottom), leptons, standard model, strong force, weak force responsible for beta decay
- **Energy applications**: Nuclear power (~10% world electricity), aircraft carriers and submarines, medical isotope production, space probes (RTGs)
- **Safety and sustainability**: Reactor safety systems, radioactive waste management, proliferation concerns, comparison with fossil fuels and renewables

**Vary the complexity based on age:**
- **For younger ages** ({{age}} < 16): Focus on qualitative understanding - E=mc² conceptually, fission splits heavy nuclei releasing energy, fusion combines light nuclei releasing energy, compare energy scales, understand chain reactions conceptually
- **For middle ages** ({{age}} 16): Calculate energies using E=mc² with given masses, understand mass defect qualitatively, write balanced nuclear equations, compare fission vs fusion energetically, analyze reactor components
- **For older ages** ({{age}} >= 17): Calculate mass defects and binding energies from atomic masses, determine energy per nucleon, analyze binding energy curve, calculate specific energy releases in fission/fusion, evaluate reactor physics, discuss quark composition

**Use appropriate formats:**

**LaTeX for formulas:**
- Mass-energy equivalence: $E = mc^2$ where $c = 2.998 \times 10^8$ m/s
- Atomic mass unit: $1 \text{ u} = 1.66054 \times 10^{-27}$ kg $= 931.5$ MeV/c²
- Mass defect: $\Delta m = (Z \cdot m_p + N \cdot m_n) - m_{\text{nucleus}}$
- Binding energy: $E_B = \Delta m \cdot c^2$
- Binding energy per nucleon: $\frac{E_B}{A}$ where $A$ = mass number
- Nuclear masses: $m_p = 1.00728$ u, $m_n = 1.00867$ u, $m_e = 0.000549$ u
- Block format for calculations:

$$\Delta m = (92 \cdot 1.00728 \text{ u} + 143 \cdot 1.00867 \text{ u}) - 235.0439 \text{ u} = 1.9151 \text{ u}$$

$$E_B = 1.9151 \text{ u} \times 931.5 \text{ MeV/u} = 1784 \text{ MeV}$$

**Adjust difficulty with {{difficulty}}:**
- **Easy**: Qualitative understanding of E=mc², recognize that fission splits heavy nuclei and fusion combines light nuclei releasing energy, understand chain reactions conceptually, compare energy scales (nuclear >> chemical)
- **Medium**: Calculate energy from given mass conversions using E=mc², write balanced nuclear equations for fission and fusion, identify components of nuclear reactors, compare energy released in different reactions
- **Hard**: Calculate mass defects from atomic masses, determine binding energies and binding energy per nucleon, analyze binding energy curve to explain stability, calculate specific energy releases for given reactions, evaluate reactor criticality

**Include variety in numerical values:**
- Different masses: 1 g, 1 kg, 0.001 kg, 235 g (1 mole U-235), various u values
- Various isotopes: H-2 (deuterium), H-3 (tritium), He-4, U-235, U-238, Pu-239, Fe-56, Kr-92, Ba-141
- Energy values: 200 MeV per fission, 17.6 MeV for D-T fusion, 26.7 MeV for p-p chain, 931.5 MeV per u
- Different binding energies: He-4 (28.3 MeV), Fe-56 (492 MeV), U-235 (1784 MeV)
- Mass defects: 0.0304 u for He-4, 1.9151 u for U-235, various values for different nuclei
- Power outputs: typical reactor 1000 MW, Sun 3.8×10²⁶ W
- Ensure calculations yield different energies and demonstrate vast scale of nuclear processes
