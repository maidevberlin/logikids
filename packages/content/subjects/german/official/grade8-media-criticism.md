---
id: grade8-media-criticism
name: Media Criticism
description: Critically evaluating media sources and bias
grade: 8
focus: Media literacy and critical evaluation
difficulty: medium
learning_objectives:
  - Identify bias and perspective in media sources
  - Distinguish between facts, opinions, and manipulation in media texts
  - Evaluate online sources for credibility and reliability
  - Recognize clickbait, sensationalism, and manipulative headlines
  - Understand filter bubbles, echo chambers, and algorithmic influence
  - Apply source evaluation criteria to assess media quality
  - Recognize propaganda techniques and persuasive strategies in media
problem_types:
  - Bias identification
  - Fact vs. opinion
  - Source credibility check
  - Clickbait detection
  - Filter bubble analysis
  - Headline evaluation
  - Author credibility assessment
  - Publication motive analysis
  - Cross-source verification
  - Media manipulation recognition
difficulty_guidelines:
  easy:
    - Obvious bias in clearly partisan sources
    - Simple fact vs. opinion distinctions
    - Recognition of exaggerated clickbait headlines
  medium:
    - Subtle bias through word choice and framing
    - Mixed fact-opinion texts requiring careful separation
    - Evaluation of source credibility using multiple criteria
  hard:
    - Implicit bias in seemingly neutral reporting
    - Complex propaganda techniques and manipulation strategies
    - Filter bubble effects and algorithmic influence on information access
prerequisites:
  - grade8-news-reporting
real_world_context:
  - Evaluating social media posts during election campaigns for bias and accuracy
  - Checking whether sensational health claims on Instagram are backed by science
  - Identifying clickbait headlines on news websites and video platforms
  - Comparing coverage of the same event across different news outlets
  - Recognizing when influencers are being paid to promote products or opinions
  - Investigating whether viral stories about celebrities or disasters are real or fake
  - Understanding why everyone in your friend group sees similar political content online
  - Analyzing how different newspapers frame stories about climate change
  - Spotting manipulative language in advertisements for gaming apps or fashion brands
  - Evaluating Wikipedia sources to determine if articles are well-researched
  - Recognizing when memes spread misinformation disguised as humor
  - Checking whether TikTok educational content comes from qualified experts
  - Analyzing how documentary films present their subjects and construct narratives
  - Understanding why YouTube recommendations keep showing similar viewpoints
  - Investigating the credibility of product reviews before making purchases
anti_patterns:
  - Avoid political topics requiring adult-level understanding of governance
  - Don't use examples from obscure or unfamiliar media platforms
  - Avoid requiring students to identify fake news without clear criteria
  - Don't present media criticism as absolute truth vs. lies (nuance matters)
  - Avoid examples relying on current events that quickly become outdated
