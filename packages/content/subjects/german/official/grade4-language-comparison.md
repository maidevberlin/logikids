grade: 4
focus: Metalinguistic awareness and language comparison
difficulty: medium
learning_objectives:
  - Compare German with foreign languages encountered in school and daily life
  - Recognize dialectal variations and heritage languages in the community
  - Examine common foreign words (Fremdw√∂rter) integrated into German
  - Distinguish between spoken and written language forms and conventions

  - Analyze how language choice affects meaning and understanding across contexts
  - Appreciate linguistic diversity and multilingualism as valuable resources
problem_types:
  - Cross-language comparison
  - Dialect identification
  - Foreign word recognition
  - Spoken vs. written distinction
  - Intention-effect analysis
  - Language feature comparison
  - Multilingual word family
  - Register appropriateness
  - Borrowed word usage
  - Language influence detection
difficulty_guidelines:
  easy:
    - Compare single words or simple phrases between German and one other language
    - Identify obvious dialect words versus standard German equivalents
    - Recognize common foreign words borrowed into everyday German (Computer, Pizza)
  medium:
    - Analyze how the same message differs between spoken and written German
    - Compare grammatical features or word order across two languages
    - Explain why certain foreign words have been adopted into German
  hard:
    - Evaluate how language choice affects the speaker's intention and listener's response
    - Compare language features across three or more languages or dialects
    - Analyze complex borrowing patterns or code-switching in multilingual contexts
prerequisites:
  - grade1-language-awareness
  - grade3-grammar-structures
real_world_context:
  - Noticing how English words like "Computer," "Email," and "Smartphone" have become part of everyday German vocabulary
  - Understanding why grandparents or relatives from different regions speak German with different pronunciations or use different words
  - Recognizing that writing formal emails requires different language than chatting with friends, even though both use German
  - Comparing German to English or other languages studied in school to see similarities and differences in grammar or vocabulary
  - Appreciating classmates who speak other languages at home and can switch between languages in different contexts
  - Discovering that many German words for food like "Pizza," "Pasta," and "Croissant" come from Italian and French
  - Comparing how you greet teachers versus friends and understanding when to use formal "Sie" versus informal "du"
  - Noticing cognates when learning new languages and seeing patterns like "Haus" and "house" or "Wasser" and "water"
  - Understanding regional expressions during family visits to different parts of Germany, Austria, or Switzerland
  - Recognizing borrowed words in sports and technology that show how languages influence each other over time
  - Observing how bilingual friends mix languages naturally when speaking with family versus using only German at school
  - Comparing sentence structures across languages and noticing why German verbs go at the end of some sentences
anti_patterns:
  - Avoid implying any language or dialect is superior or inferior
  - Don't require fluency beyond basic word recognition
  - Avoid complex linguistic terminology like "phoneme" or "morpheme"
  - Don't use examples that stereotype cultures or language communities
  - Avoid analyses requiring knowledge of uncommon foreign languages
