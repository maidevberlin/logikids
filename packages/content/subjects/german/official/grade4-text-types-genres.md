---
id: grade4-text-types-genres
name: Text Types and Genres
description: Recognizing and distinguishing fiction vs. non-fiction, narrative/poetic/dramatic forms, and developing media literacy
grade: 4
focus: Genre identification and media literacy
difficulty: medium
learning_objectives:
  - Distinguish between narrative, poetic, and dramatic text forms
  - Identify characteristics of fiction versus non-fiction texts
  - Recognize various non-fiction and practical text types (reports, instructions, articles)
  - Navigate library catalogs and media offerings to find specific types of texts
  - Search for information in both print and electronic media
  - Identify genre-specific features and structural elements
  - Select appropriate text types for different reading purposes
problem_types:
  - Genre classification
  - Fiction vs non-fiction sorting
  - Text feature identification
  - Media source selection
  - Information search strategy
  - Genre comparison
  - Text purpose matching
  - Structural element recognition
difficulty_guidelines:
  easy:
    - Use clearly recognizable genres (fairy tale, poem, recipe, news report)
    - Provide obvious structural markers (rhyme, dialogue format, step-by-step numbering)
    - Compare distinctly different text types with minimal overlap
  medium:
    - Include mixed or hybrid forms (narrative non-fiction, biographical stories)
    - Require identification of multiple genre features simultaneously
    - Compare similar genres requiring closer analysis (fable vs fairy tale, report vs article)
  hard:
    - Use texts with subtle genre markers or unconventional formats
    - Analyze texts that combine multiple genre elements
    - Evaluate effectiveness of genre choice for specific purposes or audiences
prerequisites:
  - grade3-text-analysis
  - grade3-reading-fluency
real_world_context:
  - Choosing between an encyclopedia, a story collection, or a how-to guide when looking for specific information
  - Recognizing that a newspaper article reports facts while a short story is imaginative fiction
  - Using library categories (fiction, non-fiction, poetry) to find books for different purposes
  - Identifying whether a text is trying to inform, entertain, persuade, or instruct based on its features
  - Searching online for information and distinguishing reliable sources from entertainment content
  - Distinguishing between recipes, assembly instructions, and game rules by recognizing their structural features
  - Identifying poetry by noticing rhyme, rhythm, and line breaks that differ from regular paragraph text
  - Recognizing biography versus autobiography when reading about famous scientists, athletes, or historical figures
  - Understanding that advertisements try to persuade while informational brochures aim to educate or inform
  - Comparing fairy tales with realistic fiction to understand how fantasy elements differ from everyday situations
  - Navigating different sections of websites or magazines by recognizing text types like interviews, reviews, or tutorials
  - Identifying drama scripts by noticing character names, dialogue format, and stage directions that guide performance
anti_patterns:
  - Avoid relying solely on formatting or visual layout for genre identification
  - Don't use obscure or regional text types unfamiliar to 9-10 year olds
  - Avoid texts requiring knowledge of literary history or adult genres
  - Don't conflate difficulty level with genre complexity
  - Avoid assuming all fiction is narrative or all non-fiction is dry factual text
