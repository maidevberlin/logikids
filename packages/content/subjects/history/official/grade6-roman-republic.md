---
id: grade6-roman-republic
name: The Roman Republic
description: Exploring Rome's mythical founding, the structure of the Republic including the Senate and social classes, and the transition from Republic to imperial rule
grade: 6
focus: Roman Republic structure and society
difficulty: medium
learning_objectives:
  - Understand the mythical founding of Rome in 753 BC and its significance in Roman identity
  - Identify the key institutions of the Roman Republic including the Senate and consuls
  - Analyze the social hierarchy of Roman society including patricians and plebeians
  - Explain the balance of power in the Roman Republican system
  - Trace the transition from Republic to Principate in the 1st century BC
  - Compare Republican values with imperial rule under Caesar and Augustus
  - Evaluate the causes and consequences of the Republic's collapse
problem_types:
  - Chronological ordering
  - Institution identification
  - Social class comparison
  - Power structure analysis
  - Cause-and-effect relationships
  - Historical source interpretation
  - Timeline construction
  - Role comparison
difficulty_guidelines:
  easy:
    - Basic identification of Senate, consuls, and social classes
    - Simple chronology of founding myth and major events
    - Recognition of patrician vs plebeian differences
  medium:
    - Analysis of how Republican institutions balanced power
    - Understanding multiple causes of the Republic's decline
    - Comparing Republic and imperial governance structures
  hard:
    - Evaluating conflicting historical accounts of Republic's fall
    - Analyzing how social conflicts shaped political developments
    - Connecting Republican structures to modern democratic systems
real_world_context:
  - Exploring the legend of Romulus and Remus to understand how myths shape national identity
  - Comparing the Roman Senate with modern parliaments to see democratic roots
  - Analyzing how social class conflicts in ancient Rome mirror modern inequality debates
  - Investigating how military generals like Caesar gained power through popular support
  - Studying Roman citizenship to understand inclusion and exclusion in societies
  - Examining how the conflict between patricians and plebeians led to political reforms
  - Comparing consuls' limited terms with modern presidential term limits
  - Analyzing propaganda coins and monuments to understand how leaders shaped public opinion
  - Exploring how Roman expansion created wealth but also social instability
  - Investigating the role of public speaking and rhetoric in Roman political life
  - Studying gladiator games as political tools to win popular favor
  - Examining how economic inequality contributed to the Republic's transformation
  - Analyzing assassination as a political weapon through Caesar's death
  - Comparing Augustus's careful image management with modern political branding
  - Exploring how Romans balanced tradition with the need for political change
anti_patterns:
  - Avoid requiring detailed knowledge of all Roman emperors and dates
  - Don't confuse the Republic period with the later Empire period
  - Avoid presenting patricians and plebeians as completely separate groups
  - Don't oversimplify the fall of the Republic to a single cause
  - Avoid language or scenarios unfamiliar to 11-12 year olds
