# Cultural Knowledge - Daily Life

Create tasks that help students explore and understand daily life in English-speaking countries.

**Task Requirements:**
- Compare school systems (subjects, schedules, uniforms)
- Explore eating habits and meal times
- Learn about housing and family life
- Understand geographic basics of UK and USA
- Use authentic materials (photos, schedules, menus)
- Encourage cultural comparison without judgment
- Make connections to students' own lives
- Age-appropriate content for 9-10 year olds

**Generate varied task types:**

## Variation 1: School Day Comparison
Present a typical school schedule from a British or American school. Students compare it with their own schedule and identify differences.

**Example structure:**
> Here is Emma's school day in London:
>
> | Time | Subject |
> |------|---------|
> | 9:00 - 9:45 | English |
> | 9:45 - 10:30 | Maths |
> | 10:30 - 10:45 | Break |
> | 10:45 - 11:30 | Science |
> | 11:30 - 12:15 | PE |
> | 12:15 - 13:00 | Lunch |
> | 13:00 - 13:45 | History |
> | 13:45 - 14:30 | Art |

Ask students to:
- Find {{number}} differences between Emma's school day and their own
- Answer questions: When does Emma have lunch? Which subject comes after break?
- Discuss: What surprises you about Emma's school day?

## Variation 2: Vocabulary Matching - British vs American
Create matching exercises with everyday words that differ between British and American English.

**Example:**
Match the British words with their American equivalents:

| British English | American English |
|----------------|------------------|
| 1. {{british_word_1}} | A. {{american_word_a}} |
| 2. {{british_word_2}} | B. {{american_word_b}} |
| 3. {{british_word_3}} | C. {{american_word_c}} |
| 4. {{british_word_4}} | D. {{american_word_d}} |

Common pairs: *lorry/truck, flat/apartment, biscuit/cookie, lift/elevator, football/soccer, autumn/fall, trainers/sneakers*

## Variation 3: Meal Time Exploration
Present information about typical meals and eating times in English-speaking countries.

**Example structure:**
> In the UK, people usually eat:
> - **Breakfast** (7:00-8:00): cereal, toast, eggs
> - **Lunch** (12:00-13:00): sandwiches, often called "packed lunch"
> - **Dinner** or **Tea** (18:00-19:00): main hot meal
> - **Supper**: sometimes a light snack before bed

Questions:
- What do British people call their evening meal? (*dinner* or *tea*)
- When do they usually eat lunch? (around {{time}})
- What's a typical breakfast food in the UK?
- Compare: How is this different from meal times in your country?

## Variation 4: Geography Quiz
Test knowledge of basic geographic facts about the UK and USA.

**Example questions:**
1. The capital of the United Kingdom is _____.
   - A) London
   - B) Manchester
   - C) Birmingham

2. How many countries make up the United Kingdom?
   - A) Two
   - B) Three
   - C) Four

3. The United States has _____ states.
   - A) 48
   - B) 50
   - C) 52

4. Match the city to the country:
   - New York: _____
   - Edinburgh: _____
   - Los Angeles: _____
   - Cardiff: _____

## Variation 5: Housing and Family Life
Present information about typical housing and ask comprehension questions.

**Example:**
> Many families in Britain live in **terraced houses** or **semi-detached houses**. Children often share bedrooms with siblings. Most houses have a small garden, which British people call a *garden* (Americans call it a *yard*). In cities, many people live in *flats* (British) or *apartments* (American).

Tasks:
- What's the British word for what Americans call an "apartment"? (*flat*)
- True or False: All British houses have large gardens. (False)
- Fill in: British people call it a *garden*, Americans call it a _____.
- Draw or describe: What does a terraced house look like?

## Variation 6: Cultural Symbols and Icons
Identify and learn about cultural symbols from English-speaking countries.

**Example:**
Match the symbol to the country:
1. Big Ben → _____
2. Statue of Liberty → _____
3. Double-decker bus → _____
4. Yellow school bus → _____
5. Red telephone box → _____

Questions:
- Which city can you find Big Ben? (London)
- What color are traditional British telephone boxes? (*red*)
- Name {{number}} famous landmarks in the USA or UK

## Variation 7: School Uniform Discussion
Compare school uniform traditions in different countries.

**Example:**
> In the UK, most schools require students to wear uniforms. A typical uniform includes:
> - Boys: trousers, shirt, tie, blazer
> - Girls: skirt or trousers, blouse, tie, blazer
> - Everyone: school shoes (often black)
>
> In the USA, most public schools don't require uniforms. Students can wear casual clothes like jeans and t-shirts.

Questions:
- Do most British schools have uniforms? (Yes)
- Do most American schools have uniforms? (No)
- What might a British school uniform include? ({{items}})
- Opinion: What do you think about wearing school uniforms? Why?
